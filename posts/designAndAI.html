Adapting to AI as a Designer

Jakob Nielsen and an "Urgency around AI"

It all started when I read an article titled "Design Needs an Urgency for AI." It is written by jakob nielsen (who has had a steeak of very bad takes as i’m writing this. But, as somone who is actually worried baout what might happen and how i might be bale to keep my job it stressed me out. It complains that engineers are taking charge and defining what AI is right now, instructing designers to start using AI and learning it without offering concrete advice on what it entails. So, I went searching the internet to figure out what could actually be useful to learn as a designer.

What does it mean to "Learn AI"?

Luckily, I came across an article from a Grumpy designer who wonders what it means to learn AI Right now, we're at a starting point, something that's been thrown out a lot is becoming a proficient designer in prompt engineering. He sees this as something designers should excel at because it's about articulating what you actually want to do, and it's about communication skills. But he also points out, and i agree with this, is that this is a short term opportunity. right now writing good prompts is very important but as this field matures this is one of the things it will try to improve the most on. We expect all these AI models to get better and better at understanding what people can do better, so there's a short period when we'll have professions like this.

The other thing he suggests is that one can use APIs to build on ideas, and this could be a killer feature right now. Learning how to connect to an API and build valuable things on top, might be the most promising course forward imo. The problem is that this requires a skillset quite different from what you would expect from a “designer” designer. Of course, being able to do research and identify users painpoints will always be helpful but research skills have been downprioritised in the designers arsenal during the last decade or so.

The last advice, and i saw many others give this advice, is to just start using AI tools and become good at it. there is a learning curve to be able to craft the right prompt to get what you want. this is very common advice but i’m unhappy with it. we are essentially telling designers to learn tools in a situation when the tools will most likely not last. What one needs to learn is how to understand how to get what they want from an AI, how to generate prompts, and guide so that you get a useful result.

It is not a resilient skill since it requires constant upkeep and the expertise you gain is the very thing the service providers will try to automate away.

This Forbes article emphasizes the importance of understanding generative AI, focusing on its underlying mechanics. While this insight may not be universally necessary, they proceed to discuss its potential impact on job security. Contrary to the notion that AI threatens only specific roles, it's evident that automation affects various job markets. Therefore, it's essential to assess the core skills of your profession and determine their susceptibility to AI automation. Even roles with minimal exposure to automation should consider incorporating generative AI into their workflow to stay relevant.

The article suggests two approaches to navigate the evolving job landscape: embracing AI or focusing on human-centric skills. While mastering AI tools is one option, cultivating skills like creativity, critical thinking, leadership, empathy, and relationship-building can also future-proof your career. By focusing on these areas, individuals can enhance their value in roles less likely to be automated by AI.

How might designers stay competetive?

Then, I read this article titled "How Might Designers Stay Competitive in the AI Revolution." They argue that there are three different use cases for AI right now (i want to dig deeper into the applications for ai later):

  • Provide human-like answers
  • Generate content
  • Generate graphics
Since AI will be able to quickly and cheaply create mockups for visual design they, kinda, advice against anything that has to do with pushing pixels. The advice is to become proficient in these tools and increase one's own efficiency to become a productive designer with these new tools. However, they also believe that one should start working on their social intelligence and focus more on human-centered design.

Becoming proficient with these tools will help you keep up but will not give you and edge in my opinion.

A somewhat different perspective comes from a source discussing dilemma of graphic design. They start by stating that we expect there to be two types of special tools: one is AI co-pilot, and the other is fully automated. over time we will move to the fully automated version but right now we are in a co-pilot stage. I really want to dig deeper into these metaphors later These models have become quite good at generating raster graphics with pixels, but there are still very few that are used for vector graphics and curves. They say that it may be because algorithmically, it has been seen that they have not seen as many applications for this. Here is something that could be taken, they see a very large opportunity for style transfer for language generation, especially when it comes to producing fonts for languages that are very different, such as Mandarin and Japanese.

As designers face a crossroads, either we use AI to become more creative and efficient, or we can let AI and its quality of our work become so much easier to produce both images and fonts. When that happens, we will have very low-quality images, so designers can still have an edge here by having good taste and knowing what good design actually looks like, and not using bad design elements. i think this is proably the most robust skill for designers: being able to identify and produce high quality work. with the prolifferantion of AI for design there will be a lot of garbage out there. they talk about it in the context of fonts but it hink this is true in general. They conclude with a speculation that we might use design and text on web pages more like custom writing rather than fonts which is a very interesting future.

So... to sum up

The urgency is still there and the takes on how to adapt to AI are not really satisfying to me. The advice to 'just start playing around with the tools' feels reactive to me. It lacks an analysis on how things are changing. I suppose 'move with the times' is not bad advice but could we maybe be a bit more strategic?

I hope to follow up on this but for now i guess i'll have to join with the rest and hope that simply by engaging with it I will adapt to it...
